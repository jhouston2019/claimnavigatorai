How to Write an Insurance Supplement That Recovers $8,000-$35,000

The difference between a paid supplement and one that gets ignored often comes down to structure. Here's the exact format that gets claims adjusted.

⚠️ Vague supplement requests get denied. Itemized requests with documentation get paid. The structure of your letter matters as much as the numbers.

Why Supplement Letter Structure Matters

For the complete supplement process—from documenting gaps to escalation—see our supplement master guide.

Insurance adjusters process dozens of claims weekly. They prioritize supplements that are easy to evaluate: clear itemization, attached proof, and professional formatting. Supplement letters that are emotional, vague, or poorly organized get pushed to the bottom of the pile—or denied with a form letter.

When you write a supplement that mirrors the format insurers use internally, you signal competence. That significantly increases the likelihood of a full or partial payment. Policyholders who use structured supplement templates recover an average of $12,000-$28,000 more than those who submit informal emails.

The Five Elements Every Supplement Letter Must Have

  1. Header with claim identification — Policy number, claim number, date of loss, and your contact information. This ensures your supplement reaches the right file.
  2. Subject line — "Supplement Request - Claim #[number] - [Date] - Additional $[amount]." Clear and searchable.
  3. Brief introductory paragraph — State that you're formally requesting a supplement based on your review of the estimate and contractor bids. Reference your policy's obligation to pay for necessary repairs.
  4. Itemized schedule — The core of the letter. Each line: description, carrier amount (if any), requested amount, supporting document reference. Total at the bottom.
  5. Closing with deadline — Request a response within 15-30 days. State that you'll escalate if you don't receive a written response.

How to Build the Itemized Schedule

The itemized schedule is what gets you paid. For each additional amount, include:

  • Line reference — If the carrier used line numbers, reference them. "Line 45 - O&P not included" or "New item - Code upgrade insulation."
  • Description — Clear explanation of what the item is and why it's necessary.
  • Amount requested — Exact dollar figure. No ranges.
  • Supporting document — "See Contractor A estimate, page 2" or "See permit fee schedule attached."

Example format:

Item 1: Overhead and Profit (10% O, 10% P) on subs — $4,200. Supporting: Contractor estimate, industry standard per policy. Attached.

Item 2: Code upgrade - roof decking 7/16" to 1/2" OSB — $1,850. Supporting: Building code section 202.1, Contractor B estimate. Attached.

Supporting Documentation to Attach

Contractor Estimates

At least two detailed estimates showing complete scope and market pricing

Photos

Date-stamped images of damage supporting each disputed item

Code / Permit Docs

Building code citations, permit fee schedules, engineering if required

Never submit a supplement without attachments. The letter makes the case; the attachments prove it. Carriers will not pay based on your word alone.

Step-by-Step: Writing Your Supplement Letter

Step 1: Complete Your Line-by-Line Comparison

Before writing, compare the carrier estimate to contractor estimates. Document every missing item, underpriced line, and quantity error. Create a spreadsheet or use comparison software. Your itemized schedule comes directly from this analysis.

Step 2: Draft the Itemized Schedule

List each supplement item with description, amount, and document reference. Use a table format for clarity. Total the column. This schedule becomes the heart of your letter.

Step 3: Write the Letter Wrapper

Add the header, subject line, intro paragraph, and closing. Keep the letter itself to one page. The schedule and attachments do the heavy lifting.

Step 4: Compile Attachments and Submit

Assemble contractor estimates, photos, code docs in PDF. Send via email to your adjuster and claims email. Consider certified mail for a paper trail. Note the date for follow-up.

Common Supplement Letter Mistakes

Being Emotional or Aggressive

Threats and anger get your file flagged, not paid. Maintain a professional, factual tone. Let the documentation carry the weight.

Requesting Round Numbers Without Backup

"We need another $20,000" with no itemization will be denied. Every dollar must tie to a specific line item with supporting proof.

Failing to Reference Policy Language

When applicable, cite your policy's obligation to pay for necessary repairs, code upgrades, or market rates. Policy language strengthens your position.

Frequently Asked Questions

What should an insurance supplement letter include?

A supplement letter must include your policy and claim number, a clear subject line, an itemized list of each additional amount requested with line-by-line documentation, supporting evidence (contractor estimates, photos, code citations), and a professional tone. End with the total additional amount and a reasonable response deadline.

How long should an insurance supplement letter be?

Supplement letters typically run 2-4 pages plus attachments. The letter itself should be concise; the power is in the itemized schedule and supporting documentation. Each line item should have a description, requested amount, and reference to the attached proof.

Can I write a supplement letter myself or do I need a lawyer?

You can write a supplement letter yourself. Most successful supplements use professional templates and clear documentation—no legal degree required. Lawyers are only necessary when the carrier acts in bad faith or you're considering litigation.

What format do insurance companies expect for supplements?

Carriers expect a formal business letter with claim number in the subject/reference line, an itemized schedule matching their estimate format (line numbers if available), contractor estimates, photos, and any code or permit documentation. PDF format is standard.

How do I calculate the additional amount to request in a supplement?

Compare your carrier estimate line by line to contractor estimates. For each missing item, use the contractor's price. For underpriced items, document the market rate and request the difference. Sum all line items for your total supplement amount.
