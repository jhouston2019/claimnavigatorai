Overview

Fire claims involve multiple perils—heat, smoke, soot, and water from suppression. Cause and origin investigations inform coverage, subrogation, and safety. Insurers often deploy origin-and-cause experts; policyholders may engage independent experts when findings conflict. Scope must address structural damage, HVAC contamination, contents cleaning vs. replacement, and specialty cleaning for electronics and textiles.

Smoke infiltration can impact hidden cavities, insulation, and ductwork; testing and clearance protocols are key. Water used to extinguish fires can create secondary moisture and mold risks if not dried promptly. Separate structural, contents, and additional living expense/business interruption components for clarity.

Inventory requirements are rigorous: list items, age, condition, brand, and values. Salvage decisions should be documented; some items require specialized restoration. Keep chain-of-custody for any evidence tied to subrogation potential.

Timely mitigation (board-up, tarping, drying) protects coverage and reduces disputes. Request carrier estimates and vendor scopes, and reconcile line by line with your contractors.

Key Rules & Requirements

  • Identify all damage types: heat, charring, smoke, soot, and water.
  • Preserve evidence relevant to cause and origin; avoid disturbing key areas until documented.
  • Comprehensive cleaning protocols may include HVAC cleaning, sealing, and deodorization.
  • Contents inventories must be detailed; cleaning vs. replacement decisions need justification.
  • Mitigation (board-up, tarping, drying) is required to prevent further loss.

Step-by-Step Guidance

  1. Secure the site: board-up, tarp, and ensure utilities are safe.
  2. Request carrier inspection and origin-and-cause evaluation; document your own photos immediately.
  3. Begin drying and soot containment; obtain environmental testing if smoke impact is significant.
  4. Develop separate scopes for structure and contents, including cleaning vs. replacement decisions.
  5. Track additional living expenses or business interruption impacts with receipts and logs.

Required Documentation

  • Cause-and-origin reports and fire department documents.
  • Structural and contents inventories with condition, age, and pricing.
  • Mitigation invoices (board-up, drying, cleaning) and environmental test results.
  • Contractor estimates and proof of repairs; ALE/BI expense logs and receipts.

Common Insurer Tactics

  • Limiting smoke cleaning to visible areas while excluding hidden cavities.
  • Applying aggressive depreciation to contents without considering condition.
  • Delaying ALE/BI payments pending structural scope resolution.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Pressure to discard items before they are inventoried or evaluated.
  • Refusals to test HVAC or interior cavities despite heavy smoke exposure.
  • Estimates omitting code upgrades or specialty trades (electrical, HVAC, restoration).

Best Practices for Policyholders

  • Maintain control of inventories; photograph and label rooms before removal.
  • Request detailed cleaning protocols and clearance criteria in writing.
  • Keep ALE/BI documentation organized and submit regularly for reimbursement.
