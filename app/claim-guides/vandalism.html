Overview

Vandalism claims involve intentional damage to property—broken windows, graffiti, forced entry, or destruction of contents. Prompt police reporting is typically required by policy; obtain the report number and officer contact. Coverage may vary if the property is vacant or under construction, so confirm policy conditions and any special endorsements.

Document forced entry indicators (pry marks, broken locks, damaged frames) and the path of damage inside. Capture serial numbers and photos for damaged contents. Mitigation—board-up and securing the premises—should occur immediately to prevent further loss.

Distinguish vandalism from theft; some policies treat them differently, and sublimits may apply. If business personal property is impacted, inventory losses with purchase details. For buildings, provide contractor estimates to restore damaged components.

Insurers may question causation if access was unsecured or the property was vacant beyond a stated period. Provide evidence of occupancy, alarm usage, and security steps to support coverage.

Key Rules & Requirements

  • File a police report promptly; keep the report number and date.
  • Show signs of forced entry or intentional damage to establish peril.
  • Mitigate immediately: board-up, change locks, and secure openings.
  • Check vacancy clauses and endorsements that may limit vandalism coverage.
  • Inventory building and contents damage separately with valuations.

Step-by-Step Guidance

  1. Notify law enforcement and obtain a report number; share it with the carrier.
  2. Photograph exterior and interior damage, focusing on forced entry points.
  3. Secure the property and prevent further loss through board-up or lock replacement.
  4. Compile contents and structural estimates; include receipts or pricing sources.
  5. Submit claim documentation and respond promptly to any requests for additional detail.

Required Documentation

  • Police report and incident number.
  • Photos of forced entry, damaged property, and serial numbers where available.
  • Contractor estimates for repairs and board-up invoices.
  • Contents inventory with values and proof of ownership where possible.

Common Insurer Tactics

  • Questioning forced entry to suggest non-covered causes.
  • Applying vacancy exclusions or conditions to limit payment.
  • Reducing valuations due to alleged pre-existing damage.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Carrier hesitation to accept the police report or requests to delay board-up.
  • Assumptions that missing items were stolen rather than vandalized without investigation.
  • Denials citing vacancy without verifying occupancy facts.

Best Practices for Policyholders

  • Keep security system records or occupancy proof to counter vacancy arguments.
  • Provide organized inventories and repair scopes with photos to speed evaluation.
  • Maintain professional communication and request written explanations for any adverse decision.
