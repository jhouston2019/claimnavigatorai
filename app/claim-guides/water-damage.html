Overview

Water damage claims turn on cause (sudden and accidental vs. repeated seepage), category (clean, gray, black), and documentation of mitigation. Policies often exclude long-term leakage and limit mold coverage. Prompt drying, moisture mapping, and photo logs are essential to prove reasonableness and prevent secondary damage.

Distinguish between resulting damage (covered) and faulty work or wear-and-tear (often excluded). Sudden pipe bursts are typically covered; slow leaks may be denied. Insurers expect mitigation actions such as shutoff, extraction, dehumidification, and removal of saturated materials when necessary.

Mold limitations usually cap coverage and require rapid response. Maintain drying logs, equipment readings, and contractor invoices. If hidden damage is discovered, notify the carrier immediately and request reinspection.

Contents and structural claims should be separated, with detailed inventories and scope notes. Waterline staining, delamination, and microbial growth risk should be documented with dates to show progression.

Key Rules & Requirements

  • Coverage depends on sudden and accidental cause; long-term leakage is often excluded.
  • Mitigation must begin promptly—document actions and readings.
  • Mold sublimits may apply; note caps and required approvals for remediation.
  • Hidden damage discoveries should be reported immediately to adjust the scope.
  • Separate dwelling and contents claims with clear inventories and valuations.

Step-by-Step Guidance

  1. Stop the source and photograph conditions before mitigation begins.
  2. Engage mitigation contractors; keep moisture maps, daily readings, and equipment logs.
  3. Notify the carrier, request inspection, and share initial findings.
  4. Develop a repair scope once materials are dry; include flooring, drywall, cabinetry, and finishes.
  5. Submit supplements for hidden damage discovered during tear-out with dated photos.

Required Documentation

  • Cause-of-loss description with photos and video.
  • Drying logs, moisture readings, and mitigation invoices.
  • Repair estimates and material specifications.
  • Contents inventory with values and proof of ownership where available.

Common Insurer Tactics

  • Labeling the loss as “long-term seepage” to deny coverage.
  • Rejecting mitigation scope as excessive without offering alternatives.
  • Applying mold sublimits broadly to non-mold repairs.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Delayed inspections allowing conditions to worsen.
  • Demands for tear-out without agreeing to restore or pay resulting damages.
  • Denials that ignore moisture logs or expert findings.

Best Practices for Policyholders

  • Start mitigation immediately and keep thorough, dated records.
  • Communicate discoveries in real time to expand scope properly.
  • Request written explanations for any denial of mitigation or mold-related items.
