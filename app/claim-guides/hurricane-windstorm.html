Overview

Hurricane and windstorm claims often involve multiple perils: wind, wind-driven rain, and potential flood. Anti-concurrent causation clauses and separate hurricane/wind deductibles can dramatically impact recovery. Distinguishing wind damage (covered) from flood (often excluded or separately insured) is critical; roof uplift indicators, creased shingles, and wind-created openings support wind causation.

Post-storm inspections should document elevations, debris lines, and interior patterns of intrusion. Storm-created openings (shingle loss, flashing failure, impact damage) can establish coverage for ensuing rain. Moisture mapping helps separate wind-driven water from ground-up flood.

Business interruption and additional living expense claims should tie displacement or revenue loss to covered wind damage. Civil authority and ingress/egress coverage may apply when orders restrict access.

Catastrophe environments bring surge staffing; request adjuster continuity and written scopes. Keep independent contractor estimates and engineer evaluations where structural uplift or envelope failure is suspected.

Key Rules & Requirements

  • Identify and document wind-created openings; connect interior damage to those points.
  • Differentiate wind vs. flood; note waterlines, debris patterns, and entry points.
  • Review anti-concurrent causation clauses and hurricane/wind deductibles carefully.
  • Ordinance or Law may apply for roof decking, strapping, and code upgrades.
  • Maintain ALE/BI logs tied to covered wind damage and access restrictions.

Step-by-Step Guidance

  1. Photograph exterior and roof conditions early; capture missing shingles, lifted seams, and impact points.
  2. Document interior moisture paths and elevations; use moisture readings to map spread.
  3. Request carrier inspection; if disputed, consider independent roofing/engineering assessments.
  4. Prepare a detailed repair scope including roof, exterior cladding, windows, and interior finishes.
  5. Track ALE/BI impacts with receipts and financial records; submit periodically for reimbursement.

Required Documentation

  • Roof and exterior photos showing uplift, creases, and openings.
  • Moisture maps, interior damage photos, and mitigation invoices.
  • Contractor or engineer reports supporting wind causation.
  • ALE/BI expense logs, revenue records, and supporting invoices.

Common Insurer Tactics

  • Attributing interior water to flood or pre-existing leaks despite wind indicators.
  • Applying high hurricane deductibles even when events do not trigger them.
  • Limiting roof repairs to spot fixes where full replacement is warranted by uplift or brittle failure.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Denials without on-roof inspection or without testing for brittle shingles.
  • Ignoring window/door failures or wind-driven rain paths when scopes are written.
  • Payment offers that exclude ordinance/code-required upgrades.

Best Practices for Policyholders

  • Capture thorough roof documentation before emergency tarping when safe.
  • Keep separate files for wind vs. flood evidence; clarify coverage triggers.
  • Request written deductible application explanation and coverage position tied to facts.
