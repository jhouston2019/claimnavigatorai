The Problem

David R. filed a claim for wind damage to his roof. The insurance carrier approved the claim and issued a payment of $26,300.

David hired a contractor and completed the roof replacement. The total cost: $42,500.

David expected the insurance company to reimburse the difference. He submitted his contractor's final invoice and completion photos to the adjuster.

Three weeks passed with no response. David called the adjuster multiple times and was told the claim was "under review."

After 6 weeks, David received a letter stating his claim was "closed" and no additional payment would be issued.

David didn't understand. His policy had a Replacement Cost Value (RCV) endorsement. He had completed the repairs and submitted proof. Why wasn't the depreciation being released?

The withheld amount: $16,200.

Understanding ACV vs. RCV

Most homeowner policies with RCV endorsements pay claims in two stages:

Stage 1: Actual Cash Value (ACV) Payment

The initial payment equals the Replacement Cost Value minus depreciation. This is called the Actual Cash Value (ACV).

For David's roof:

  • Replacement Cost Value (RCV): $42,500
  • Depreciation (based on age and condition): $16,200
  • Actual Cash Value (ACV): $26,300

The carrier paid David $26,300 (ACV) upfront.

Stage 2: Recoverable Depreciation Payment

After the policyholder completes repairs, the carrier releases the withheld depreciation. This is called the Recoverable Depreciation payment.

To receive the depreciation, the policyholder must submit:

  • Proof of completed repairs (contractor invoice, receipts)
  • Photographic documentation of completed work
  • Signed completion certificate or lien waiver from contractor

David submitted his contractor's invoice and photos. But the carrier refused to release the depreciation.

Why the Carrier Withheld Payment

We reviewed David's submission and identified the problem: incomplete documentation.

The carrier's depreciation recovery requirements (buried in the policy endorsement) specified:

  1. Itemized contractor invoice showing labor and materials
  2. Proof of payment (canceled check or credit card statement)
  3. Before-and-after photographic documentation
  4. Signed completion certificate from contractor
  5. Lien waiver or release from contractor
  6. Building permit and final inspection approval (if required by local code)

David had submitted only the contractor's invoice and completion photos. He was missing:

  • Proof of payment
  • Signed completion certificate
  • Lien waiver
  • Building permit documentation

The carrier used the incomplete submission as grounds to deny the depreciation recovery.

The Documentation Strategy

Step 1: Policy Review

We reviewed David's policy and identified the specific depreciation recovery requirements in the RCV endorsement.

Key findings:

  • Depreciation must be claimed within 180 days of ACV payment
  • Policyholder must provide "satisfactory proof" of completed repairs
  • Carrier has 30 days to issue payment after receiving complete documentation

David was still within the 180-day window. He had 4 weeks remaining to submit complete documentation.

Step 2: Documentation Checklist

We provided David with a depreciation recovery checklist:

  1. Itemized contractor invoice: Must show labor, materials, and total cost. Generic invoices are insufficient.
  2. Proof of payment: Canceled check, credit card statement, or wire transfer confirmation showing payment to contractor.
  3. Completion certificate: Signed statement from contractor confirming work is complete and meets building code.
  4. Lien waiver: Signed release from contractor waiving right to file mechanic's lien.
  5. Before-and-after photos: Timestamped photos showing pre-repair damage and post-repair completion.
  6. Building permit documentation: Copy of permit and final inspection approval from local building department.

Step 3: Depreciation Recovery Demand

We provided David with a depreciation recovery demand template that included:

  • Cover letter citing policy RCV endorsement and depreciation recovery provisions
  • Checklist of submitted documentation
  • Policy citation establishing 30-day payment deadline
  • Notice of intent to file complaint with state insurance department if payment is not issued within deadline

Common Depreciation Recovery Mistakes

Mistake #1: Incomplete Documentation

Most policyholders submit only the contractor's invoice. Carriers require comprehensive proof of completion, including lien waivers, permits, and proof of payment.

Solution: Review the policy's RCV endorsement to identify specific documentation requirements before submitting depreciation recovery request.

Mistake #2: Missing the Deadline

Most policies require depreciation recovery claims within 180 days of the ACV payment. If the deadline is missed, the depreciation is forfeited.

Solution: Track the 180-day deadline from the date of the ACV payment. Submit depreciation recovery request at least 30 days before the deadline to allow time for carrier review.

Mistake #3: Accepting Partial Depreciation

Some carriers release only partial depreciation, claiming the actual repair cost was less than the RCV estimate. This is often incorrect—the policy entitles the policyholder to the full RCV amount if repairs are completed to code.

Solution: If the carrier releases only partial depreciation, submit a demand letter citing the policy's RCV provision and providing contractor invoice showing actual costs met or exceeded the RCV estimate.

Mistake #4: Not Following Up

Carriers often delay depreciation payments, hoping policyholders will forget or miss the deadline. David's carrier ignored his initial submission for 6 weeks.

Solution: Establish a clear payment deadline in the depreciation recovery demand (typically 30 days per policy terms). Follow up weekly if payment is not issued within the deadline.

Lessons Learned

1. Read the RCV Endorsement

The depreciation recovery requirements are specified in the policy's RCV endorsement, not the main policy document. Most policyholders never read this section and submit incomplete documentation.

2. Collect All Required Documentation Before Submitting

Incomplete submissions give carriers grounds to deny depreciation recovery. Collect all required documentation (invoice, proof of payment, lien waiver, permits) before submitting the recovery request.

3. Track the 180-Day Deadline

Depreciation recovery deadlines are strictly enforced. If the deadline is missed, the depreciation is forfeited—even if repairs were completed. Track the deadline from the date of the ACV payment and submit the recovery request at least 30 days before expiration.

4. Establish Clear Payment Deadlines

Carriers often delay depreciation payments indefinitely. Citing the policy's payment timeline (typically 30 days) and establishing a clear deadline forces timely processing.

5. Don't Accept Partial Depreciation Without Challenge

If the carrier releases only partial depreciation, demand the full amount with supporting contractor invoice. The policy entitles you to the full RCV amount if repairs are completed to code.
