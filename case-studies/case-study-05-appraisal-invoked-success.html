The Problem

Michael T. owned a two-story residential property in suburban Dallas that sustained significant damage during a severe hailstorm in May 2025. The storm produced golf ball-sized hail and sustained winds exceeding 70 mph, causing extensive roof damage, siding damage, and multiple window failures.

His insurance carrier, a regional provider with a reputation for aggressive claim denials, sent an adjuster within 7 days. The adjuster spent approximately 90 minutes on-site, took photos of the roof from ground level, and verbally indicated that "most of the damage appears cosmetic."

Four weeks later, Michael received a settlement offer: $22,800.

Michael obtained three contractor estimates ranging from $58,000 to $67,000. The insurance estimate covered only partial roof repairs, excluded all siding damage, and denied coverage for window replacements—claiming pre-existing wear and tear.

The gap: $41,200 minimum.

Michael attempted to negotiate with the adjuster for three weeks. The adjuster refused to schedule a reinspection and repeatedly stated that the estimate was "final and non-negotiable." The carrier's claims supervisor did not respond to Michael's emails or phone calls.

Facing a stalemate, Michael invoked the appraisal clause in his policy—triggering an independent valuation process that ultimately resulted in a $64,000 appraisal award.

What Was Missing

The insurance adjuster's estimate contained multiple critical omissions and coverage denials:

  • Siding damage denied: Adjuster claimed hail impacts on vinyl siding were "cosmetic only" and not covered. Contractor documented functional damage including cracking and water intrusion risk.
  • Window replacements denied: Adjuster claimed broken seals were "pre-existing wear and tear" despite visible hail impact points on frames.
  • Underestimated roof scope: Adjuster estimated 40% roof replacement. Contractor documented 100% failure of shingle seals due to hail impact—requiring full replacement per manufacturer warranty requirements.
  • No code upgrade coverage: Local building code required enhanced decking attachment for wind resistance. Adjuster excluded this as "betterment."
  • Garage door excluded: Adjuster claimed dents were "cosmetic." Contractor documented structural damage preventing proper operation.
  • Skylights excluded: Adjuster did not inspect roof-mounted skylights. Both units sustained cracked glazing from hail impact.

The Documentation Strategy

Step 1: Policy Analysis

We reviewed Michael's HO-3 homeowner's policy. Key findings:

  • Coverage A (Dwelling): $385,000 limit
  • Replacement Cost Value (RCV) endorsement—no depreciation on structural components
  • Ordinance or Law coverage: $75,000 (covers code-mandated upgrades)
  • Appraisal clause: Either party may invoke appraisal to resolve valuation disputes

The policy covered "direct physical loss" from windstorm and hail, with no exclusion for functional siding damage, window damage, or code upgrades required by permit. The adjuster's denials were coverage interpretations, not policy exclusions.

Conclusion: All contractor-identified repairs were covered. The carrier's denials were improper and subject to appraisal.

Step 2: Evidence Collection

We provided Michael with a comprehensive evidence checklist:

  1. Independent roof inspection: Hire a licensed roofing contractor to perform detailed roof inspection with close-up photos of hail damage, shingle seal failures, and decking damage.
  2. Siding inspection report: Obtain written report from siding contractor documenting functional damage, water intrusion risk, and manufacturer warranty implications.
  3. Window inspection: Hire glazing specialist to document hail impact points and failed seals. Obtain report confirming damage was storm-related, not pre-existing.
  4. Building code documentation: Contact local building department to obtain written confirmation of code requirements for roof decking attachment and permit requirements.
  5. Weather data: Obtain NOAA storm report and local weather station data confirming hail size and wind speeds on date of loss.
  6. Contractor estimates: Obtain three detailed, line-item estimates from licensed contractors with scope descriptions and material specifications.

Michael completed this documentation within 14 days, spending approximately $1,200 on independent inspections and reports.

Step 3: Structured Demand and Appraisal Notice

We provided Michael with a formal demand letter template citing policy provisions and attaching all supporting documentation. The letter established a 15-day response deadline per policy terms.

The carrier responded after 18 days with a form letter stating the estimate was "final" and offering no additional payment.

Michael then invoked appraisal using our appraisal invocation letter template. The letter:

  • Cited the policy's appraisal clause verbatim
  • Identified the disputed amount ($41,200)
  • Named Michael's chosen appraiser (a licensed public adjuster with appraisal experience)
  • Demanded the carrier name its appraiser within 20 days per policy terms
  • Noted that failure to participate in appraisal could constitute bad faith under state law

Negotiation Tactics Encountered

Throughout the claim process, the insurance carrier employed several aggressive tactics to avoid payment:

Tactic #1: Ground-Level Roof Inspection

The adjuster inspected the roof from ground level using binoculars and did not physically access the roof surface. This is a common cost-containment practice—adjusters avoid detailed inspections to minimize documented damage.

Counter-strategy: Michael's roofing contractor performed a detailed roof inspection with close-up photos of hail impacts, shingle seal failures, and decking damage. The appraisal process required both appraisers to physically access the roof, confirming the contractor's findings.

Tactic #2: "Cosmetic Damage" Denial

The carrier claimed siding damage and garage door dents were "cosmetic only" and not covered. This is a misinterpretation of policy language—policies cover "direct physical loss," not just damage that affects structural integrity.

Counter-strategy: Michael's siding contractor documented functional damage including cracking, water intrusion risk, and manufacturer warranty violations. The appraisal award confirmed that functional damage was covered, regardless of whether it was "cosmetic."

Tactic #3: Pre-Existing Damage Claim

The carrier denied window replacements, claiming failed seals were "pre-existing wear and tear." This is a common tactic to avoid payment for expensive components.

Counter-strategy: Michael's glazing specialist documented hail impact points on window frames and confirmed that seal failures were caused by storm impact, not age. The appraisal process confirmed storm causation.

Tactic #4: Refusal to Negotiate

The carrier refused to schedule a reinspection, ignored Michael's demand letter, and stated the estimate was "final and non-negotiable." This is an intimidation tactic designed to force policyholders to accept lowball offers.

Counter-strategy: Michael invoked appraisal, which is a contractual right that cannot be denied by the carrier. The appraisal process bypassed the carrier's negotiation stonewalling and resulted in an independent, binding valuation.

The Appraisal Process

What Is Appraisal?

Most property insurance policies include an appraisal clause that allows either party to demand an independent valuation of the loss when the parties cannot agree on the amount of damage.

The appraisal process works as follows:

  1. The policyholder invokes appraisal by written notice to the carrier
  2. Each party selects an independent appraiser
  3. The two appraisers select a neutral umpire
  4. The appraisers inspect the property and prepare independent valuations
  5. If the appraisers agree, their valuation is binding
  6. If the appraisers disagree, the umpire issues a binding award
  7. The carrier must pay the appraisal award (minus any amounts already paid)

Appraisal is not arbitration. Appraisal resolves only the amount of the loss, not coverage disputes. However, in practice, appraisers often resolve scope disputes (what damage is covered) as part of valuing the loss.

Why Appraisal Worked in This Case

Appraisal was the optimal resolution strategy for several reasons:

  • Carrier refused to negotiate: The carrier's "final and non-negotiable" stance made further negotiation futile. Appraisal bypassed the negotiation deadlock.
  • Clear policy coverage: The disputed items (roof, siding, windows) were clearly covered under the policy. The dispute was about valuation, not coverage—making it ideal for appraisal.
  • Strong documentation: Michael's independent inspections and contractor estimates provided a solid foundation for his appraiser's valuation.
  • Cost-effective: Appraisal cost Michael $2,400 (his appraiser's fee), which he recovered from the carrier per policy terms. Hiring an attorney would have cost $15,000-$25,000 in fees.
  • Binding result: The appraisal award was binding on both parties. The carrier could not appeal or refuse payment.

Lessons Learned

1. Appraisal Is a Powerful Tool

When carriers refuse to negotiate, appraisal provides a contractual mechanism to force independent valuation. Most carriers settle immediately when appraisal is invoked to avoid the cost and bad-faith exposure.

2. Ground-Level Inspections Are Inadequate

Adjusters who inspect roofs from ground level cannot accurately assess damage. Policyholders should hire contractors to perform detailed inspections with roof access and close-up documentation.

3. "Cosmetic Damage" Is Often Covered

Carriers frequently deny damage as "cosmetic only." However, policies cover "direct physical loss"—not just damage that affects structural integrity. Functional damage, manufacturer warranty violations, and water intrusion risk are all covered losses.

4. Pre-Existing Damage Claims Require Proof

Carriers often claim damage is "pre-existing" without evidence. Policyholders can rebut this by obtaining expert reports confirming storm causation and documenting the absence of prior damage.

5. Documentation Drives Appraisal Outcomes

Appraisers base their valuations on documented evidence. Michael's independent inspections, contractor estimates, and expert reports provided the foundation for his appraiser's $67,500 valuation—resulting in a $64,000 award.

6. Appraisal Costs Are Recoverable

Most policies cover appraisal costs as a claim expense. Michael recovered his $2,400 appraiser fee from the carrier, making the appraisal process cost-neutral.
