Georgia Insurance Complaint Guide: File with OCI

Step-by-step guide to filing complaints with Georgia Department of Insurance for storm claims, denied claims, and bad faith disputes.

Georgia Department of Insurance (OCI) Contact Information

Online Complaint Form: oci.georgia.gov

Phone: 1-800-656-2298 (Consumer Services)

Mail: Georgia Department of Insurance, Consumer Services Division, 2 Martin Luther King Jr. Drive, Suite 704, West Tower, Atlanta, GA 30334

Hours: Monday-Friday, 8:00 AM - 5:00 PM Eastern Time

When to File a Georgia OCI Complaint

File a complaint with the Georgia Office of Insurance and Safety Fire Commissioner when your insurance company violates Georgia law or your policy terms. Common triggers for Georgia policyholders:

  • Storm or tornado claim denial without investigation — Carrier denies wind or hail damage without proper inspection
  • Unreasonable delay — Carrier misses Georgia-mandated deadlines for acknowledgment, investigation, or payment
  • Lowball settlement offers — Offer is 30-50% below documented repair costs with no justification
  • Refusal to negotiate in good faith — Carrier ignores demands, won't respond to documentation, or makes take-it-or-leave-it offers
  • Misrepresentation of policy terms — Carrier claims coverage doesn't exist when policy language clearly provides it
  • Bad faith tactics — Intimidation, threats to cancel policy, or coercive settlement pressure
  • Failure to pay undisputed amounts — Carrier withholds payment on portions of claim that are clearly covered

Georgia Bad Faith Law: What Qualifies

Georgia law defines bad faith under O.C.G.A. §33-4-6. To prove bad faith in Georgia, you must show the carrier refused to pay without any reasonable grounds. Bad faith in Georgia includes:

  • Denial without reasonable basis — Denying coverage when facts and policy clearly support payment
  • Failure to investigate properly — Not conducting adequate investigation before denial
  • Unreasonable delay — Delaying payment without legitimate reason
  • Lowball offers without justification — Offering significantly less than documented damages without explanation
  • Misrepresenting policy terms — Claiming exclusions that don't apply or coverage that doesn't exist
  • Refusing to settle when liability is clear — Not paying undisputed amounts promptly
  • Failure to provide written explanation — Not explaining denial or valuation basis in writing

Georgia requires showing the carrier knew or recklessly disregarded that it had no reasonable basis for denial. Bad faith claims can result in compensatory damages, attorney fees, and in some cases, punitive damages.

Georgia Storm Claim Protections

Georgia experiences frequent severe weather including tornadoes, hail, and wind storms. Georgia law provides protections for storm victims:

  • Prompt investigation required — Carriers must investigate storm claims promptly and thoroughly
  • Reasonable payment deadlines — Once investigation is complete, carriers must pay or deny within reasonable time
  • Wind vs. hail disputes — Carrier must provide engineering support if attributing damage to excluded peril
  • Code upgrade coverage — Many Georgia policies include ordinance or law coverage for required upgrades
  • Matching requirements — Some policies require matching undamaged portions when repairs affect appearance

If your storm claim was denied or underpaid, these protections strengthen your OCI complaint.

Step-by-Step: How to File a Georgia OCI Complaint

Step 1: Gather Your Documentation

Before filing, compile a complete documentation package. OCI complaints with strong evidence produce better outcomes.

  • Policy declarations page and relevant policy sections
  • Claim number and date of loss
  • All correspondence with the carrier (emails, letters, adjuster notes)
  • Carrier's estimate and any denial letters
  • Contractor estimates with line-item breakdowns
  • Photos and videos of damage (before and after any repairs)
  • Timeline of key events (storm date, claim filed, adjuster visit, estimate received, demands sent)
  • Proof of storm damage (weather reports, news coverage)
  • Any demand letters you've sent

Step 2: File Your Complaint Online or by Phone

Online (recommended): Visit oci.georgia.gov and navigate to the consumer complaint section. Complete the online form with:

  • Your contact information
  • Insurance company name and policy number
  • Claim number and date of loss
  • Detailed description of the problem (be specific: what happened, when, what you've tried, why the carrier's position is wrong)
  • Upload supporting documents (estimates, photos, correspondence)

By phone: Call 1-800-656-2298. An OCI representative will help you file and may request documents by email or mail.

By mail: Download the complaint form from OCI's website, complete it, and mail with copies of supporting documents to the address above.

Step 3: OCI Reviews and Contacts the Carrier

After you file:

  1. OCI acknowledges receipt — Usually within 2-5 business days via email or mail
  2. OCI forwards complaint to carrier — Carrier has 20 days to respond in writing
  3. Carrier must provide written explanation — Must address each issue you raised and provide documentation
  4. OCI reviews carrier's response — Determines if carrier violated Georgia law or policy terms

Step 4: OCI Investigation and Resolution

OCI may:

  • Require corrective action — If carrier violated law, OCI can order compliance
  • Facilitate settlement discussions — Regulatory pressure often prompts better offers
  • Refer to enforcement — Serious or repeated violations may result in fines or sanctions
  • Close complaint if no violation found — OCI provides explanation

You receive copies of all correspondence. Most complaints resolve within 30-90 days.

Escalation Timeline: When to Take Each Step

Escalation Pathway

Days 1-30: Negotiate directly with adjuster. Submit formal demand with documentation. Allow 15-30 days for response.

Days 30-45: If no movement, escalate to claims supervisor. Request written explanation of valuation or denial.

Days 45-60: If still no resolution, file OCI complaint. Regulatory pressure often changes carrier's position.

Days 60-90: OCI investigates. Carrier must respond. Many claims settle during this period.

Days 90+: If OCI complaint doesn't resolve, consider appraisal (for valuation disputes) or consult attorney (for coverage or bad faith issues).

What Georgia OCI Can and Cannot Do

OCI can:

  • Investigate unfair claim practices
  • Require carriers to respond in writing
  • Order corrective action for violations
  • Impose fines and sanctions for repeated violations
  • Create regulatory pressure that prompts better offers
  • Refer serious violations to enforcement

OCI cannot:

  • Order a specific settlement amount
  • Force the carrier to pay your claim
  • Act as your lawyer or adjuster
  • Award damages or attorney fees

For payment disputes over amount, consider invoking your policy's appraisal clause. For coverage denials or bad faith, consult a Georgia insurance attorney.

After Filing: What to Expect

Once you file a Georgia OCI complaint:

  • Carrier attention increases — Complaints are tracked and affect carrier ratings. Many carriers settle quickly to avoid regulatory scrutiny.
  • Written responses required — Carrier must explain their position in writing, which often reveals weaknesses in their case.
  • Settlement offers may improve — Regulatory pressure frequently prompts better offers within 2-4 weeks of filing.
  • Timeline clarity — OCI provides structure and deadlines, preventing indefinite delays.
  • Documentation of bad faith — OCI correspondence creates a record useful if you later pursue litigation.

Keep copies of all OCI correspondence. If the carrier increases their offer, evaluate it carefully before accepting. You can always negotiate further or pursue appraisal/litigation if needed.

Georgia-Specific Claim Requirements

Georgia law imposes specific requirements on carriers:

  • 15 days to acknowledge claim — Carrier must acknowledge receipt within 15 days of notification
  • Prompt investigation — Must begin investigation promptly after acknowledgment
  • Reasonable time to pay or deny — Once investigation is complete, carrier must pay or deny within reasonable time (typically 30-60 days)
  • Written explanation required — All denials must include specific reasons and policy references
  • Payment within reasonable time after agreement — Once amount is agreed, payment due promptly

If the carrier misses these requirements without reasonable cause, that's grounds for an OCI complaint and may support a bad faith claim.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Filing too early — Try negotiation and supervisor escalation first. OCI is most effective when you've exhausted direct negotiation.
  • Incomplete documentation — Weak complaints produce weak results. Build your evidence package before filing.
  • Vague descriptions — Be specific: what happened, when, what you've tried, why the carrier is wrong, what policy language supports your position.
  • Not documenting storm damage — For storm claims, prove the damage occurred during the storm with photos, weather data, and timeline.
  • Expecting OCI to award money — OCI investigates violations but doesn't order specific payments. Use appraisal or litigation for payment disputes.
  • Not following up — Check your email and mail regularly. Respond promptly to OCI requests for information.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I file a complaint with the Georgia Department of Insurance?

File online at oci.georgia.gov or call 1-800-656-2298. You'll need your policy number, claim number, insurer name, and detailed description of the issue. Georgia OCI typically responds within 30-60 days.

What qualifies as bad faith in Georgia?

O.C.G.A. §33-4-6 defines bad faith as refusal to pay without reasonable grounds. Georgia courts require showing the carrier had no reasonable basis for denial and knew or recklessly disregarded lack of basis. Bad faith includes unreasonable delay and lowball offers.

How long does Georgia OCI take to resolve complaints?

Most Georgia OCI complaints receive initial response within 15 business days. Full investigation typically takes 30-90 days depending on complexity. Carriers must respond to OCI within 20 days of notification.

Can Georgia OCI force my insurance company to pay my storm claim?

OCI cannot order a specific settlement amount but can investigate unfair practices and require corrective action. Regulatory pressure often prompts better offers, especially for storm and tornado claims.

What documentation do I need to file a Georgia insurance complaint?

Gather: policy documents, claim correspondence, adjuster estimates, contractor bids, photos of damage, timeline of events, proof of storm damage (if applicable), and any demand letters sent.

Will filing a complaint affect my insurance rates?

No. Georgia law prohibits carriers from raising rates or canceling policies in retaliation for filing complaints. If you experience retaliation, file an additional complaint with OCI.

What happens after I file a Georgia OCI complaint?

OCI reviews your complaint, contacts the carrier, and requests a written response. The carrier must respond within 20 days. OCI investigates and may require corrective action. You receive copies of all correspondence.

Can I file a complaint if my storm claim was denied?

Yes. If the denial was improper, lacked investigation, or violated policy terms, OCI can investigate. Include documentation showing storm damage and why the denial violated policy terms.

Should I hire a lawyer before filing a Georgia OCI complaint?

Not required. Most policyholders file complaints themselves. However, if the claim involves significant money, bad faith, or complex coverage issues, consulting a Georgia insurance attorney can help.

What is the deadline to file a Georgia OCI complaint?

No specific deadline, but file as soon as possible. Georgia has a 6-year statute of limitations for breach of contract and 4 years for bad faith claims. Don't delay—regulatory pressure is most effective early.
